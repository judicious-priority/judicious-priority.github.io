The text of the two subsections establishes this dynamic: • Subdivision (a) states: "There shall be no motions for post-trial relief in any domestic relations matter..." • Subdivision (b) states: "A party aggrieved by the decision of the court may file a motion for reconsideration..." The apparent contradiction lies in why the rule bans post-trial motions but immediately allows reconsideration motions. The key to understanding this is that they are two entirely different legal mechanisms with distinct procedural burdens. ________________________________________ 1. Why Subsection (a) Bans "Post-Trial Relief" In a standard civil lawsuit (like a car accident case), a party must file a formal "Motion for Post-Trial Relief" within 10 days of a verdict to preserve their right to appeal. This triggers a lengthy, mandatory briefing and argument process. The Supreme Court of Pennsylvania explicitly banned this in family law matters because custody cases require speed. If custody orders were automatically put on ice for months while post-trial motions were argued, children would be trapped in unstable, long-term legal limbo. Subsection (a) completely cuts out this mandatory middle step. Once the judge signs a custody order, it is instantly final and active. 2. Why Subsection (b) Allows "Reconsideration" Because subsection (a) strips away the standard civil process for asking a judge to fix an error, subsection (b) provides a safety valve: the Motion for Reconsideration. The critical difference is that a motion for reconsideration is entirely optional and discretionary. • It does not stop the custody order from taking effect. • It does not stop your 30-day appeal clock from ticking away. • The judge has absolute authority to look at the motion and ignore or deny it without holding a single hearing. No, completely optional in family court. Pauses Appeal? Yes, automatically stops the clock until decided. No. The clock keeps running unless the judge signs an explicit order. Child Custody • The Status Quo Remains Active: Filing a motion under this rule does not stay the underlying custody schedule. Child and Spousal Support • Financial Accumulation: While a support order is undergoing reconsideration, financial obligations and support arrears continue to accrue based on the numbers in the challenged order. • Retroactivity: If the trial court ultimately grants reconsideration and lowers or raises the support obligation, the adjusted financial figures are typically applied retroactively back to the original date of the initial support filing. 3. Protection From Abuse (PFA) • Immediate Safety Enforcement: Granting a motion for reconsideration does not suspend an active PFA injunction. No. 1930.2 Under Pennsylvania law, the right to seek reconsideration of a domestic relations order is not restricted by a party's formal alignment as Plaintiff or Defendant. Instead, the rule extends this procedural right to any active participant whose legal rights are directly impacted by the court's decree. 1. The "Aggrieved Party" Standard Under Pa.R.C.P. No. 1930.2(b), the explicit right to file a motion for reconsideration is granted to "a party aggrieved by the decision of the court." To be considered an "aggrieved party" in Pennsylvania, a person must meet three specific criteria established by appellate case law: • Adverse Interest: The party must have a substantial, direct, and immediate interest in the outcome of the litigation. • Harm or Injury: The court's order must have directly caused them a legal or financial injury, or stripped them of an established legal right. • Not in Privity with the Prevailing Party: The party cannot be someone who fully won their claims; they must be the party whose requested relief was denied in whole or in part. 2. Application to Specific Parties • The Defendant: If the court enters an adverse custody, support, PFA, or PFSV order against the Defendant, the Defendant is an aggrieved party and has the full right to file for reconsideration. • The Plaintiff: If the court denies the Plaintiff's requested relief (e.g., denying a PFA petition or granting less custody time than requested), the Plaintiff becomes the aggrieved party and possesses the identical right to file under the rule. • Intervenors: A third party who has been formally granted intervenor status under Pa.R.C.P. No. 1915.6 (such as a grandparent seeking custody rights) is treated as a full party to the record. If an order harms their specific claims, they hold the independent right to file for reconsideration as an aggrieved party—provided their claims have not legally abated.
